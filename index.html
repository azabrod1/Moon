Every planet and moon trails an invisible cone of shadow away from the Sun; an eclipse is one body passing through another’s cone. This draws the cones of the system you’re in at true scale — so during an event you can see the moon sitting inside its planet’s shadow, or its own shadow touching the planet.
True positions · lines widened to stay visible
Annotates the moon’s real path like a textbook ellipse: nearest and farthest points, plus the two foci — the planet sits at one (Kepler’s first law). The shaded sectors sweep equal areas in equal times, which is why the moon runs faster when it’s closer (the second law).

Welcome to Your Planetarium

Everything you see is in its real place. Planets, moons, and the stars match the actual sky for the date on screen, at real scale.

Two speeds

You can fly up to 20c between planets. The moment you enter a planet's system, the ship throttles way down so you don't blast straight through it. Want to stay fast? Flip Slower near planets off in the menu.

Go anywhere (🚀 Teleport)

The 🚀 button up top opens the deck: every planet and moon, one tap each. The tab decides what the tap does — Teleport drops you right next to the body, Autopilot flies you there (grab the controls and it lets go), and Observatory lands you on it. Type to search instead of scrolling.

Landing

Get close to a body and a Land on… button appears on its own. Drag to look around. Leave (or Esc) puts you back in the ship.

Stand on a world (🔭 Observatory)

Tap 🔭 and pick any planet or moon — you'll land there. Its sky panel shows the current phase, distances, and the next eclipses and shadow crossings, all worked out from the orbits; a switch at the bottom of the picker decides whether the panel opens on arrival by itself. Tap an event to jump to it, then Surface view to watch from the ground.

Watch the orbits run

Click the date on the bottom bar. Speed time up and you can actually watch planets trace their orbits and moons circle their hosts. Pause, reverse, or jump to any UTC moment.

How many fit?

How many Earths would fill Jupiter? Pour real planets into a glass one and find out. It's in the Tools menu.

Have fun out there.
